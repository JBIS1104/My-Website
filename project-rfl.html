The challenge

Design a robot that wins by making the other one lose.

Antweight class: maximum 150 g. Arena: a flat combat box. Objective: immobilise opponents or push them out. Rules are tight; ingenuity is the differentiator.

My approach: if the robot is impossible to get underneath, and it can fit completely underneath its opponent — it wins. That became the design philosophy.

Design philosophy

Wedge wins.

A perfectly flat wedge at near-zero ground clearance slides under opponents before they can react. A low centre of gravity means it’s extremely hard to flip.

The robot needed to be mechanically reliable above all else — in combat, complexity is a failure point. “Space Ship” is deliberately simple: strong geometry, low profile, high torque.

Engineering

Design decisions.

Near-zero ground clearance

The chassis underside is milled flat to within fractions of a millimetre. This prevents opponents from wedging underneath — one of the most common ways combat robots are flipped.

Ultra-low centre of gravity

All heavy components (batteries, motors) are mounted as low and centrally as possible. This makes the robot resistant to toppling even under heavy side impact.

Wedge geometry

The front face angles forward and downward, creating a ramp that guides under opponents and lifts them, reducing their traction and exposing vulnerable underside components.

AutoCAD from scratch

The entire robot was designed in AutoCAD, iterating through geometry to balance the wedge angle, weight distribution, and motor placement within the 150 g class limit.
