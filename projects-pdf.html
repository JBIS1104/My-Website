01 — Head-Tracking Monitor Arm 3rd Year Project · 2025 – Current

A servo-driven monitor arm that follows the user’s head in real time — no manual adjustment ever needed.

User NeedChronic friction of manual repositioning
ConceptClosed-loop face-tracking system
DesignRRR vs RPR kinematic study in Fusion 360 & AutoCAD
PrototypeiPad MJPEG → face detect → hardware PWM on RP5
ValidateOngoing — iterating to full build

Problem identified: conventional monitor arms require constant manual adjustment as users shift posture. The friction is invisible but persistent — and entirely solvable with sensing and actuation.

I designed a closed-loop mechatronic system: an iPad streams live video via MJPEG over HTTPS to a Raspberry Pi 5, which ingests each frame, runs an OpenCV Haar cascade face detector to locate the face, computes the pixel offset from frame centre, and drives two servo axes directly via hardware PWM on GPIO pins 18 and 13 using a dead-band filter to suppress jitter. The loop runs continuously — stream → detect → compute offset → PWM → repeat.

Design exploration: two joint configurations — RRR (three revolute joints) and RPR (revolute-prismatic-revolute) — were modelled and compared in Fusion 360 and AutoCAD to evaluate workspace coverage, mechanical complexity, and load path. Component assemblies (base, elbow, body, head) were iterated across multiple CAD versions.

Design Engineering relevance: This project exemplifies the systems integration challenge at the heart of mechatronics — hardware sensing, real-time software control, and mechanical structure must be co-designed. Every design decision traced back to eliminating a clearly defined user friction point.

03 — Line-Following Buggy Academic Project · 2024 – 2025

End-to-end mechatronics design — CAD, FEA, hardware build, soldering, and PID control — with the full mechanical report authored personally.

RequirementsSpeed, tracking accuracy, structural load
DesignSolidWorks CAD + FEA validation
BuildHardware assembly, wiring & soldering
CodeC/C++ PID, experimentally gain-tuned
ValidatePre-heats tests passed; heats attempts failed due to sensor failure

This team project covered the full mechatronic development cycle. My personal contributions spanned every domain: physical assembly of chassis and drivetrain; full parametric CAD of structural components; FEA on critical load-bearing members; all electrical wiring, motor driver connection, and soldering; a C/C++ PID control loop tuned experimentally; and the complete mechanical section of the final project report.

Hardware: the buggy is built around an STM32F401RE (mbed) microcontroller. The sensor array uses 6× TCRT5000 IR sensors on a custom PCB — common-emitter configuration, 75 Ω current-limiting and 4.7 kΩ output resistors for full 3.3 V ADC range, arc-shaped to fit the ball castor and detect curves earlier. Background subtraction (LED on − LED off) removes ambient light interference. The chassis was redesigned with the battery pack positioned between the gearboxes to lower the CG, and an upper strut-brace (inspired by the BMW M3 strut tower brace) provides torsion resistance. Plastic standoffs and screws saved ~80 g. A HM-10 BLE module triggers the 180° turnaround via Bluetooth.

Control: a state machine manages line-following, line-lost, stopping, and turnaround states. The PID uses trapezoidal integration, anti-windup, and low-pass filtering. Gains were tuned sequentially (P → D → I) across multiple base speeds.

Heats outcome: a sensor failure before competition reduced the array from 6 to 4 sensors, creating blind spots. All three timed attempts failed — losing the line after turnaround (attempt 1), at the first curve (attempt 2), and at the bottom curve after a successful ramp climb (attempt 3). The team did not qualify for the final race, though the mechanical design and control algorithm performed well in pre-heats testing and most TD4 marks were secured.

Design Engineering relevance: Demonstrates the full design-build-test-validate cycle with structured analysis at each stage. FEA informed geometry before manufacture; PID tuning followed a systematic method, not trial-and-error. Technical writing communicated the rationale clearly to the assessor.

04 — Robot Fighting League — “Space Ship” UoM Robotics Society · 2024 – 2025

An Antweight (150 g) combat robot built from a single strategic insight — wedge wins — validated in live competition.

StrategyArena rules analysed → wedge dominance identified
ConceptZero ground clearance + ultra-low CG
DesignAutoCAD from blank sheet, 150 g budget
OptimiseWeight, geometry, motor placement iterated
CompeteOpponents lifted in live competition bouts

Design rationale from first principles: I began by analysing the Antweight ruleset and common failure modes. The insight: a robot that cannot be wedged under, and can wedge under its opponent, wins structurally. That became the non-negotiable constraint around which everything else was designed.

The chassis underside was machined flat to near-zero ground clearance. A forward-angled wedge face generates lift on contact. All heavy components — batteries and motors — were placed as low and central as possible to resist toppling. The entire assembly was designed in AutoCAD from blank sheet with weight-budget tracking through every revision to remain within the 150 g class limit. Space Ship successfully lifted opponents in live competition.

Design Engineering relevance: Defined by constraint-led creativity — a hard mass limit forced rigorous trade-off decisions at every step, and the winning design emerged from a clear strategic framing of the problem. Complexity was deliberately avoided; reliability under impact was the only metric that mattered.
Design philosophy derived from arena rule analysis — not aesthetics or convention

05 — Video Generation for Ethically Aligned Design Independent Project · 2026

Exploring how generative AI video output can be steered toward ethical intent — prompt engineering as a design discipline.

IntentDefine ethical message & audience
Prompt DesignStructured briefs in Google AI Studio
GenerateVeo 3.1 video pipeline
RefinePost-edit to close intent gap
ReflectAI authorship implications for design

This project sits at the intersection of design thinking and AI literacy. I explored whether — and how — the output of a generative video model can be steered to communicate a specific ethical position, rather than producing generic or unintended content.

The workflow: structured prompts built iteratively in Google AI Studio, passed to Veo 3.1 for generation, followed by a deliberate post-editing pass to close the gap between generated output and intended message. The process revealed that prompt engineering is a form of design authorship — the quality of output depends entirely on the clarity and intentionality of the brief.

Design Engineering relevance: Generative AI is reshaping the design process. Understanding how to direct these tools — and recognising their limits — is a core competency for the next generation of design engineers. This project demonstrates critical engagement with emerging tools rather than passive use, which aligns directly with Imperial’s innovation and entrepreneurship focus.
